# Misunderstood 
My name is Marcus James Brewer, and I was born in Yuba City, California.
The story of my life did not begin with stability. It began with broken pieces—pieces of memory, pieces of family, and pieces of a childhood that was changed before I was old enough to understand why.
I was about five years old when I was taken from my parents.
At that age, I didn’t understand what addiction was. I didn’t understand why adults made the choices they made. I didn’t understand court orders, Child Protective Services, or family separation. I only knew that my life was changing, and I knew it in the worst way a child can know anything: through fear, confusion, and loss.
My father, James Brewer, was always in and out of jail. Looking back now, I can see he was fighting battles of his own, but as a little boy I didn’t see it that way. I just knew he wasn’t always there. I knew there were times when he disappeared from my life and times when he came back around, but nothing ever felt steady.
My mother, Denise Robison, is harder for me to remember from those early years. I was so young that a lot of those memories are just fragments now. What I do know is that both of my parents were on drugs and unstable. The environment around me wasn’t healthy, and eventually the state stepped in.
One day, I was no longer living with my parents.
Just like that, the life I knew was gone.
I wish I could tell you I remember every detail of that day, but I don’t. What I remember most is the feeling. The confusion. The uncertainty. The fear that comes when a child realizes something is happening that he cannot stop.
After I was removed from my parents, I went to live with my grandmother.
For a little while, it felt like maybe things would be okay.
Grandma was family. Even though everything had changed, there was still some comfort in being around someone who knew me and cared about me. At that age, that mattered more than anything. A familiar face. A familiar voice. A place that didn’t feel completely foreign.
But life had another turn waiting for me.
My grandmother was older, and caring for a young child wasn’t easy. Eventually, CPS said I had to go somewhere else. I didn’t understand the reasons. I didn’t understand why one home could suddenly become temporary. All I knew was that another move was coming. Another goodbye. Another chapter ending before I had a chance to settle in.
Not long after that, my mother’s sister stepped in.
My aunt came and got me.
I don’t remember the trip itself. I don’t remember being packed up or driven away. That part of my life is missing from my memory, buried somewhere I can’t reach. Maybe I was too young. Maybe too much was happening all at once. Maybe my mind protected me by letting go of the details.
What I do know is that by 1995, we were in Colorado.
That’s where my life really started to take shape in a way I would never forget.
We moved into this beat-up little ghetto house in a place known as the Red Brick Projects. That neighborhood was rough. It was nothing but gang bangers and drugs. The kind of place where danger felt normal and chaos was just part of the air you breathed. It wasn’t the kind of place a child should have to grow up in, but that was my reality.
We lived there for about two years.
Two years might not sound like a long time to some people, but when you’re a kid living in a place like that, two years can feel like forever. I was still young, still trying to understand the world, still trying to figure out where I belonged. But the Red Brick Projects taught me things no child should have to learn so early. It taught me how to stay alert. It taught me how to read the energy around me. It taught me that not every place that looks like home is safe.
There were people around who were caught up in their own mess. Drugs were everywhere. Violence wasn’t far behind. You could feel the tension in the neighborhood. You could see it in the way people carried themselves. You could hear it in the way folks talked, the way they watched each other, the way they moved through life like they were always bracing for something.
That was the world I was dropped into.
And even though I was just a kid, I could feel that something about my life was already different from other kids’ lives.
Then my mother started getting her life together.
That part of the story matters to me because it shows that people can change. My mother got clean. She started doing better. My aunt let her move in, and for a long time she really did good. She stayed clean. She held a job. She was trying to build something better for herself and for us.
As a child, I didn’t fully understand how hard that must have been. I just knew things felt different. There was a shift in the house. A little more stability. A little more hope. A little less of the chaos that had followed us before.
For a while, it seemed like maybe life was finally turning in the right direction.
Then she met this guy named Jerry.
Jerry was a real piece of shit, if you ask me.
That’s the honest truth.
He came into the picture and brought his own problems with him. Some people walk into a family and make things better. Some people walk in and leave damage behind. Jerry was the second kind. He wasn’t the kind of man who brought peace into the home. He brought his own mess, his own attitude, his own bullshit.
But even with all that, there’s one thing I can say about him: in the end, the only good thing he brought into this family were Lala and Juju.
Those would be my little sisters.
And no matter what else happened, no matter how much I disliked Jerry or what kind of man he was, I can’t deny that my sisters are one of the best things that came out of that part of my life. Lala and Juju became a bright spot in a story that had already seen too much darkness. They are awesome to this day, and I love them for that.
Family is complicated like that.
Sometimes the people who hurt you are tied to the people you love. Sometimes the worst chapters of your life still lead to something beautiful. Sometimes the same road that brought pain also brought the people who helped you survive it.
As a child, though, I couldn’t see all that clearly.
I was still just a little boy trying to make sense of a world that kept changing around me.
I had already been taken from my parents.
I had already been moved from one place to another.
I had already learned that stability could disappear without warning.
And now I was living in a rough neighborhood in Colorado, watching my mother try to rebuild her life while new people came into the picture and changed the family dynamic all over again.
That kind of childhood leaves a mark on you.
It teaches you not to trust too easily.
It teaches you not to get too comfortable.
It teaches you that love can be real and still not be enough to keep everything together.
Years later, I would understand how much those early experiences shaped me. The separation from my parents wasn’t just one event. It was the beginning of a chain reaction that affected nearly every part of my life. The anger. The loneliness. The feeling of not belonging anywhere. The questions about family. The questions about myself. They all started back then.
I would grow up carrying pieces of a story I didn’t fully understand until much later.
I would spend years trying to figure out where I fit in.
I would spend years dealing with the damage that comes from being uprooted too young and too often.
And even now, when I look back, I can still see that little boy in the Red Brick Projects. I can still see him trying to survive in a world full of instability, trying to hold on to whatever pieces of family he could, trying to understand why life had already been so hard before he even had a chance to grow up.
That little boy was me.
And before the trouble, before the anger, before the streets and the prison cells, before all the mistakes that would come later, there was simply a child trying to survive the life he was given.

Chapter 2: Lost Before I Knew Why
After a few years, we moved out of the Red Brick Projects and into a nicer house. The neighborhood was safer, and I started going to a better school. From the outside, it probably looked like my life was finally getting on the right track.
But the truth was, I was still lost.
Don't get me wrong—my aunt and uncle were wonderful people. They did everything they could to give me a good life. They fed me, clothed me, gave me a roof over my head, and loved me the best they knew how.
The problem wasn't them.
The problem was that I couldn't escape the feeling that this wasn't where I belonged.
Deep down, I wanted to be with my parents, even though they couldn't take care of me. I couldn't explain those feelings because I didn't understand them myself. All I knew was that I felt empty, angry, and confused. I was carrying around hurt that a child should never have to carry.
That hurt started showing itself in everything I did.
I began acting up at school. I talked back to teachers. I got into trouble at home. Every time someone tried to help me, I pushed them away. Looking back now, I don't believe I was a bad kid.
I was a broken kid.
By the time I was nine years old, I was already smoking cigarettes, drinking alcohol, and smoking weed.
Nine years old.
When I think about that today, it breaks my heart. Kids that age should be worried about homework, cartoons, baseball cards, and riding their bikes—not trying to numb pain they don't even have the words to describe.
But that was my reality.
One day I broke into a house across the street from where we lived. I was looking for money—really, anything I could find that seemed valuable. At the time, I wasn't thinking about the consequences. I was just acting on impulse, chasing something I couldn't even explain.
The homeowners called the police.
For a while, I thought I'd gotten away with it. Weeks turned into months, and nothing happened. I figured it was over.
Then one day, the police showed up.
I was arrested.
Looking back, that arrest was more than just punishment for one mistake. It was the beginning of a cycle that would define my life for years to come. Court dates, probation officers, juvenile programs, and endless chances that I didn't know how to accept became part of my everyday life.
I was eventually sent to a residential school where I was forced to live.
I hated every minute of it.
I didn't want to be there. I ran away whenever I had the chance. Every time I escaped, I found myself right back in the same crowd, making the same bad decisions.
I was searching for somewhere to belong.
Unfortunately, I found that belonging in the streets.
The people I ran with became my family. They accepted me without asking questions. They didn't care about my past or where I came from. At the time, that felt like love.
But it wasn't.
It was a road leading straight toward destruction.
Then one night, everything changed.
I was hanging out with the homies when things got out of control. Someone got hurt.
Someone lost their life.
In a matter of moments, my childhood was over.
I was arrested and taken to the county jail.
Even though I was only a kid, I was charged as an adult.
Nothing could have prepared me for walking into that jail.
The doors slammed shut behind me, and for the first time, I truly understood that my life might never be the same. I was scared, confused, and trying to make sense of how everything had fallen apart so quickly.
I spent months fighting my case.
Every court appearance gave me a little hope that somehow things would work out differently.
They didn't.
I was found guilty.
At just fourteen years old, I was sentenced to twenty years in the Colorado Department of Corrections.
Fourteen years old.
Most kids that age are starting high school, worrying about sports, girlfriends, or what they're going to do after graduation.
I was being transported to prison.
As the reality of that sentence settled in, I realized something I never should have had to realize as a child.
My childhood was over.
There was no more room for mistakes. No more chances to pretend I was just another kid.
From that moment on, I knew I would have to grow up fast if I wanted to survive.
I had no idea that prison would become the place where I would lose parts of myself—but also begin discovering the man I could eventually become.
